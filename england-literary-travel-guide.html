A Literary Lovers' Guide to London and Beyond

October 2024 · 6 minute read
1

Bloomsbury, London

Getty Images

This London neighborhood gave the Bloomsbury Group—members of which included Virginia Woolf, E.M. Forster, and Lytton Strachey—its name. An unofficial label for the informal social group comprised of 20th century thinkers, writers, and artists who lived in the area, the Bloomsbury Group spent much of their time working and gathering in the neighborhood's lush squares. You can visit the blue plaque commemorating their lives and work at 51 Gordon Square before visiting a bust of Woolf at Tavistock Square two blocks away. Several blocks west, in Holborn, stands the Charles Dickens Museum at 48 Doughty Street. The author lived and worked here from March 1837 to December 1839, and you can see the study in which he wrote his first three novels (The Pickwick Papers, Oliver Twist, and Nicholas Nickleby) when you visit. Finish your tour with afternoon tea at Bea's of Bloomsbury.

2

Shakespeare's Birthplace, Stratford-upon-Avon

Getty Images

A two-hour drive from central London (or a day trip by bus), this quiet village features numerous tributes to its most famous son. You can walk through the house where Shakespeare was born and raised, visit his wife's home, and walks streets that would've been familiar to the playwright. Today, the town is home to the Royal Shakespeare Company, which performs locally (there are three theaters in Stratford-upon-Avon alone) and worldwide. Finish your day with fish and chips alongside the Warwickshire Avon.

3

Knightsbridge, London

Getty Images

Bordering Hyde Park to the south, this central London neighborhood is the perfect place to ground your travels. Post up in one of The Berkeley's ultra-luxe suites for a lush view of Hyde Park and make a reservation for their famous fashion-inspired Prêt-à-Portea. The hotel even offers a fashion trunk to add vintage accessories to your look before heading out. A short walk will take you to tourist hot-spots like shopping mecca Harrods and the V&A; Museum, which plays host to exhibitions on fashion (Balenciaga: Shaping Fashion), design (Barber & Osgerby), and music (Pink Floyd: Their Mortal Remains—fans of the band can partake in a special package at The Berkeley tied to the exhibit). After browsing both, take a tour through Hyde Park to visit the statue of Lord Byron at Hyde Park Corner and Peter Pan in Kensington Gardens (J.M. Barrie's home is on Bayswater Road, a 10-minute walk from the statue). After your walk, head back to The Berkeley's newly-remodeled Blue Bar (above).

4

The British Library, London

Getty Images

Steps from King's Cross station (where you can visit Platform 9¾), the British Library has the largest catalogue of items in the world, with its 150 million-plus collection including two copies of the Magna Carta, Leonardo da Vinci's personal notebook, and Jane Austen's childhood notebook. Its principal exhibition, Treasures of the British Library, is free to the public, while its blockbuster Harry Potter: A History of Magic is currently on display through February 2018.

5

University of Oxford

Getty Images

Of course, the oldest university in the English-speaking world has a rich literary history, but perhaps the figures most closely associated with the university today are Lewis Carroll, J. R. R. Tolkien, and C. S. Lewis. Join a day trip to the quiet city or explore on your own with walking tour of Tolkien and Lewis's world. If you visit before October 29, check out the excellent Which Jane Austen? exhibition at the Bodleian Libraries. Next year, Tolkien: Maker of Middle-earth opens June 1.

6

Jane Austen's House, Chawton

Getty Images

In this simple house in Hampshire (a 90-minute drive from central London today), Jane Austen spent much of the last years of her life, possibly editing three of her most famous books—Sense and Sensibility, Pride and Prejudice, and Northanger Abbey—and completing three more (Mansfield Park, Emma and Persuasion). When you visit the museum today, you can view exhibitions (41 Objects, highlighting the museum's collection, is on display to mark the 200-year anniversary of Austen's death), tour the grounds, and even take a writing workshop. A 30-minute drive southwest will take you to Winchester Cathedral, where Austen is buried.

7

Central London

Getty Images

After perusing Charing Cross Road's famous bookstores, head west to Picadilly Circus and enter the grand Hotel Café Royal (a member of The Leading Hotels of the World), which houses the Oscar Wilde Bar (above). Known as the Grill Room when it opened in 1865, the restaurant hosted the likes of Wilde and his lover, Alfred Douglas, as well as Virginia Woolf, D. H. Lawrence, and George Bernard Shaw. Today, you can take afternoon tea amidst its gilded, mirrored furnishings. Afterward, take a stroll to Adelaide Street to visit A Conversation with Oscar Wilde, a bust and bench devoted to the literary icon.

8

Shakespeare's Globe, Southwark, London

Getty Images

Take in a Shakespearean production—the theater is currently hosting Much Ado About Nothing and King Lear through October—or a modern show at this open-air playhouse, a reproduction of the Globe Theatre where Shakespeare worked during his lifetime. Since its opening in 1997, the Globe has expanded to an indoor theater, exhibition space, and education, research, and studio facilities.

9

The Brontë Parsonage, Haworth

Getty Images

This Brontë sisters—Emily, Charlotte, and Anne—lived here with their siblings from 1820 until their deaths (Emily in 1848, Anne in 1849, and Charlotte in 1855) and did much of their writing at the dining room table. In this house, Emily wrote Wuthering Heights; Charlotte Jane Eyre, Shirley, and Villette; and Anne Agnes Grey and The Tenant of Wildfell Hall. Today, the home is a museum owned and operated by the Brontë Society, and you can take a tour and see items owned by and connected to the family when you visit. A one-minute walk takes you to St Michael and All Angels' Church, where Emily and Charlotte are buried.

10

Poets' Corner, Westminster Abbey, London

Getty Images

If you're doing the traditional tourist walk of Buckingham Palace, Big Ben, and Westminster Abbey, be sure to pay your respects to Geoffrey Chaucer, Charles Dickens, Thomas Hardy, Rudyard Kipling, and Alfred, Lord Tennyson—all of whom are buried in the Poets' Corner in Westminster Abbey. Memorials to the likes of Jane Austen, the Brontë sisters, Shakespeare and Oscar Wilde also feature in this section.

11

Bath, Somerset

Bath, England

This city in western England is closely tied to Jane Austen, who lived here from 1801 to 1806 and set her books Northanger Abbey and Persuasion here. In Bath today, the Jane Austen Centre is devoted to the author. The city also hosts a yearly music and literary festival.
