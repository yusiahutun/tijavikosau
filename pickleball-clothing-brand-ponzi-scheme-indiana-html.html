Popular pickleball clothing brand CEO known as the sports 'ultimate ambassador' is accused of runnin

February 2024 Â· 5 minute read

A popular pickleball clothing brand's CEO has been accused of running a Ponzi scheme by begging 250 of his rich friends to invest millions - then refused to pay them back.  

Rodney 'Rocket' Grubbs from Brookville, Indiana, launched Pickleball Rocks in 2009 to sell a series of apparel and equipment for the sport - and soon started to convince other pickleball fans to invest in his business. 

He was described as 'pickleball's ultimate ambassador' and traveled the world to play in tournaments - and his firm had a projected revenue of $1million in 2022. 

According to IndyStar, he convinced hundreds of people from the US, Canada and Portugal to invest and hand over millions to his startup when he needed the capital.

But in January, Indiana Secretary of State's securities division issued a cease and desist order for Grubb's 'alleged fraudulent investment scheme.' He's now facing $9 million in judgments.

He is also facing four different lawsuits, including one where several investors asked a federal judge to force Grubbs into Chapter 7 bankruptcy.

But in response, he said he is too poor to even afford an attorney.

Grubbs claims that his only eligible assets are 11 rental properties worth $800,000 - as well as $150,000 in equipment and apparel from Pickleball Rocks. 

The alleged fraudster told the judge in one of the $5million investor lawsuits last year that he won't have enough funds to pay it out - especially not if the other '250 outstanding lenders' try and get money if he files for bankruptcy.  

Teri Siewert, an investor who lives in Florida, said: 'He traveled around and used his good reputation, and also the reputation of others that he surrounded himself with, in order to take advantage of good people.

'None of them were stupid. They were doctors. They were lawyers. They were professors. One was a financial consultant. She really feels terrible. That's how trusted he was in the pickleball community.'

Oklahoma-based pickleball referee Ron Ponder invested $65,000 in Grubb's pickleball venture.

He told IndyStar: 'He's very well known within the pickleball community. He's the same way with everyone. He's happy to see you, and he can't wait to tell you about stuff.'

When Grubbs kept coming to Ponder with new business ideas asking for more money, he kept giving it to him. He reflected: 'I'm like, 'Wow, I've got $65,000 with this guy,' and I trust him because it's Rodney.

'But on the other hand, I really don't know that much about him.' 

Grubbs failed to give Ponder back his first loan in time - and weeks later, he saw a post on social media outing the pickleball fanatic for fleecing them of their investments.

He said: 'I went straight from suspicious to pissed off.

'Losing that won't affect me. I won't notice that it's gone, but he's got people's life savings. Someone said to me "You're a fool." No, I'm not a fool. I trusted a friend. I trusted someone who I like, and so that's not on me. That's on him.'

Five other investors who sent the alleged 'trustworthy' pickleball enthusiast a combined total of $300,000 said they were fleeced out of their retirement funds on the promise they'd get their investments back. 

According to his investment pitch, Grubbs guaranteed a 12 percent interest rate compounded monthly, as well as a lump sum repayment after 18 months for all his investors. 

Teri and Scott Siewert, who had played against Grubbs before, gave him a $25,000 investment.

Teri said: 'He said that he had six $25,000 investment slots that would buy him $150,000 worth of apparel so he could meet the needs of this big contract.' 

Despite begging him for their money back year after year, the Siewerts were left with nothing - even after angry legal letters were sent to Grubbs. 

They were forced to post about their ordeal online - at which point more than 20 others came forward and said they had gone through similar things with Grubbs. 

Suddenly, Teri said they were paid $56,000 'to make us shut up and go away.' 

Rick Griffith, another $25,000 investor from North Carolina, also told IndyStar: 'Most of us thought we were alone. 

'You know, we took this last spot, or, replaced somebody. There's a lot of people out there.'

Grubbs has not been charged with any crime.
