Dying elephant ear plant? 10 reasons and ways to revive it

July 2024 · 6 minute read

Elephant ear plants are known for their large, striking leaves that resemble an elephant's ear. However, they are sensitive and struggle to thrive if not adequately cared for. If you've noticed your elephant ear leaves drooping and turning yellow, don't give up on them. You can revive your dying elephant ear plant with simple steps and return it to its former glory.

If you're a fan of tropical plants, the elephant ear plant's lush appearance makes it a must-have to your plants' collection. If you notice your plant looking yellowed, limp or otherwise unhealthy, it's important to take action to revive it quickly. Why are your elephant ear plants struggling, and what tips do you need to bring these beautiful plants back to life?

10 causes and tips to restore a dying elephant ear plant

Elephant ear plants make a beautiful addition to any home or garden. To revive the plant, you should identify underlying causes and take the necessary steps to address them. You can restore the plant to its full beauty and potential with proper care and attention. Here are some reasons and ways to revive it:

1. Overwatering or underwatering

Elephant ear plants are perennial and hence sensitive to overwatering, which can lead to root rot and its demise. On the flip side, underwatering causes the plants to wilt. It is possible to incorrectly get the frequency and volume of watering the plant as each season requires different watering.

To revive an overwatered or underwatered plant, check for soil drainage issues and re-pot if the soil is too compact or aerate the soil if the plant is already big and heavy. The water frequency and volume vary depending on the weather's coolness or hotness.

2. Lack of or too much direct sunlight

Elephant ear plants require bright, indirect sunlight to thrive. However, prolonged exposure to direct and full sunlight can stress the plant, leading to dehydration, leaf scorch, and leaf tip burn. Too little sunlight will inhibit the plant’s ability to photosynthesize.

You must have just enough sunlight for your plant. Let your plant be exposed to direct sunlight for 3-5 hours to fix leaf burn or scotch issues. And in case the sun’s exposure is not enough, supplement it with artificial lighting or re-position it to a brighter location.

3. Soil nutrient deficiencies

If your indoor elephant ear plant is dying and you notice many visual symptoms, such as yellowing leaves, it could mean it's time to use a soil testing kit to replenish the soil with needed nutrients. Lack of nutrients in the soil is detrimental to any plant, and your elephant ear plants are no exception.

If the colocasia leaves are yellow, but the veins are still green, then boost the soil nutrients with the right fertiliser, like NPK or organic matter. However, it's essential that your soil is imbued with the right amount of fertiliser or organic matter. Too little or too many nutrients lead to poorer soil conditions for your plant.

4. Root-bound

Dying elephant ear plants will outgrow their pots (for an indoor plant), leading to root damage and leaf discolouration. If it has outgrown its pot or the ground has rocks that inhibit further root growth, the plant struggles to absorb nutrients and water.

To revive a root-bound plant, repot into a larger container with fresh soil and check the roots often. Pruning the roots a little also helps. Regardless of an indoor or outdoor plant, ensure the soil is well aerated and lacks inhibiting large rocks. Good drainage and enough room for root growth make all the difference.

5. Temperature fluctuations

Exposing elephant ear plants to a cold draft or sudden temperature changes causes discolouration. Most tropical plants, including your colocasia, prefer warm temperatures and are sensitive to cold, making their leaves turn yellow or brown.

If you have an indoor elephant ear plant, keep it away from cold windows or air conditioning vents, and maintain a consistent temperature. Ensure you have a heater for colder months; these tropical plants prefer 70-85 degrees Fahrenheit. If it's an outdoor plant, mist and protect it from direct exposure to chilly breezes.

6. Fungal infestation

Elephant ear plants are susceptible to various diseases like bacterial leaf spots or fungal infections. Overwatering causes fungal infection to the roots, causing drooping of the leaves.

To revive the elephant ear plant from suffering from such diseases, remove affected leaves, treat them with an appropriate fungicide or bactericide, and remove the clog to avoid drainage issues.

7. Pest infestation

Aphids, spider mites, and thrips commonly attach themselves to the lower side of elephant ear plant leaves, cutting their mineral supply. If you notice your elephant ear plant drooping or haloes in the leaf, take necessary precautions to avoid its stunted growth.

You can use margosa oil on your dying elephant ear plant. Remove the pests by blasting them off with the water gun technique.

8. Soil pH imbalance stress

Elephant ear plants prefer slightly acidic soil with a pH range of 5.5 to 6.5. If the pH is too high or too low, the plant struggles to absorb nutrients and suffers from stunted growth, leaf curl or yellowing leaves.

To recover a plant with issues of soil pH imbalance, test the soil pH and adjust it as required using a pH-adjusting solution or adding organic matter such as compost or peat moss to the soil.

9. Natural life cycle

Sometimes your beautiful plant may discolour just because of how long it has lived, and you will start questioning; should I cut off dead elephant ear leaves? Yes. Removing dead or damaged leaves is generally recommended to promote the plant's health and appearance.

Yellowing is sometimes a natural process that shouldn't worry you as the large yellow leaves finally shed off. You can revive a dying elephant ear plant by cutting the yellowing leaves to allow room for new growth. However, you should have ruled out all the other reasons.

10. Improper pruning

The plants may benefit from occasional pruning to remove dead or damaged leaves and encourage new growth. However, improper pruning stresses the plant and causes it to struggle, stunting its growth.

By identifying the underlying cause of your plant dying and taking appropriate steps and strategies, you can help it recover.
