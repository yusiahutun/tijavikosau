How to explore Egypt in luxury

July 2024 · 7 minute read

The call to prayer is resounding in the air. The air itself is warm, already close, already a bit like walking through soup, and it’s just six in the morning. My husband and I are atop a small cruise ship, gliding along the Nile as hot air balloons rise on the opposite bank, gently bobbing before the sunrise. This time of day would normally be antithetical to my very soul, and yet I’ve been up at this time – the adhan just audible and the sky a dusty pink – for the last four days. And it has been absolute heaven.

You see, the early bird, in Egypt, catches not just the worm but the temples, tombs and palaces of the ancient world, all before the gawping marches of tourists and the heat of the day descend. It has easily been around 45 degrees each day of our August trip (ideal for smaller crowds, less so for the aforementioned balminess) and yet those premature wake-up calls have enabled us to be the only people walking around a two-thousand-year-old edifice, like the temple of Philae, in the almost-bearable early-morning heat.

There have been too many of these pinch-me moments to count. Egypt itself feels like the ultimate luxury: a land spoilt with so many riches of the past it feels laughable that you are allowed to simply walk through them, like some propitious time traveller. Yet there are means of travelling in this gorgeous country that can truly take it to the next level. That is what we chose to do. Elite time travel, if you will.

We began our journey in Hurghada, a luxury beach resort on Egypt’s red sea coast. Whilst the main town itself is distinctly purpose built and unprepossessing, this is a region of Egypt which boasts some of the most luxurious, yet under-the-radar (hence the appeal) hotels. In my opinion, when you are faced with an adventure across the country, it is a great idea to begin with a few days of total relaxation, and that is exactly what The Oberoi Beach Resort, Sahl Hasheesh gave us.

Oberoi

The Oberoi Sahl Hasheesh

The resort is built to resemble an old Arabic palace, with a gargantuan turquoise blue swimming pool practically spilling into the Red Sea and rooms within smatterings of mini houses across an undulating green landscape, which itself is fringed with a sandy coastline. Our room was vast and comfortable, complete with a delicious sunken tub and private terrace. It could not have kickstarted our journey any better.

Our next step was a couple of internal flights to Aswan, via Cairo – this was, thanks to EgyptAir, the one downside of our trip (do avoid flying with them at all costs if you can help it). It's surely the finest endorsement of the Old Cataract Hotel in Aswan that, even though we arrived there at midnight, without our luggage, after a delayed flight, we were still simply ecstatic. The charm of this historic hotel cannot be overstated. Built in 1899, it retains every single ounce of character from this time period. The wood framed walls are littered with black and white photographs of the famous faces who have stayed here, from Winston Churchill to Princess Diana, the hallways whisper with the soft hum of old ceiling fans and the terrace gives way to one of the most breath-taking views of the river Nile, with iconic feluccas sailing past and palm trees swaying in the breeze.

"It's laughable that you are allowed to be here... like some propitious time traveller"

We woke up the next morning, opened our bedroom door to our ground floor balcony and overlooked the inner, verdant courtyard of the hotel, before heading to the stunning 1902 restaurant for breakfast under its grand domed ceiling. This particular room was the setting for the filming of the 1978 Death on the Nile, starring Peter Ustinov and was indeed where Agatha Christie herself stayed before embarking on her own Nile river cruise, which would go on to become the ultimate inspiration for her famous novel. Retracing her steps, we felt as though we were about to embark on our own 1920's Nile Cruise, though hopefully with less fatal results…

Reunited with our luggage (big shout out to our guide Osama) we left the Cataract and were seamlessly escorted to our home for the next four days; The Oberoi Philae Luxury Nile Cruiser. Having long been the sort of traveller who avoids cruises at all cost, this decision was a surprise to me too, and yet a Nile river cruise of this ilk is a decidedly singular proposition. For starters, it is small: more like a luxury boutique hotel than a megawatt skyscraper on sea. With only 22 cabins and suites, this is a svelte, chic affair. Our suite came complete with details like an ornate chessboard table and a bathroom with a huge tub by the window so you can soak in the bubbles as the Nile floats by.

Oberoi

The Oberoi Philae Luxury Nile Cruiser

There was so much to recommend this as the ultimate luxury when exploring Egypt. The food was sincerely sublime, the cigar and drinks lounge cosy and atmospheric, the ‘rooftop’ pool gorgeous (if somewhat hard to navigate in the extreme heat) and the service friendly and warm. It feels trite to say we found ourselves genuinely befriending the staff, but between our in-jokes with our waiter Waleed and our daily towel animal creating contest with the cleaning staff, I really did start to buy in to the old adage that a cruise becomes a strange sort of floating family.

The highlight was, however, without question, the sights we were lucky enough to see. Each small group or couple is granted its own personal guide from the cruise – another glorious luxury. Ours was exemplary. We took a motor boat around the cataracts to Philae temple; which mystically appears on its own island in the Nile as you chug towards it, itself both a marvel of ancient and modern-day engineering, having been painstakingly rebuilt stone by stone by UNESCO in the 1960s after the construction of the Aswan Dam threatened to flood its original location.

Luxor temple by night

We marvelled at the mummified crocodiles of Kom Ombo, a riverside temple to the god Sobek, we got lost in the hidden passageways and floodlit chambers of the Temple of Edfu and saw the sun set beneath the obelisk of Luxor temple as the avenue of sphinxes smiled enigmatically. We travelled deep into the mountains of Luxor to spy the tombs of the

Pharaohs, from Rameses VI to Tutankhamun, and were enthralled by the vibrant, painted book of the dead that adorned each descending wall. Then there was the majesty of Karnak; a temple which felt like an ancient metropolis; with its columns craning heavenwards and its endless statues of Pharaohs and gods alike.

The tomb of Rameses VI

On our last morning, we visited the mortuary temple of Hatshepsut, one of Egypt’s most important and yet most overlooked female rulers. As I strolled around the images of this thousands-year old monarch and the traces of ancient misogynistic vandalism from her successor, I was reminded of the layers of stories we had witnessed over the last week, from the shadow of Agatha Christie to the graffiti of a Napoleonic soldier on an ancient temple wall. For all the grandeur of our ship and the splendour of our hotels, this privileged journey in the footsteps of history felt like the greatest luxury of all.
