Mastermind behind Carlos Alcaraz's rise to Wimbledon victory: Former World No. 1 Juan Carlos Ferrero

August 2024 · 6 minute read

He's been coaching 20-year-old Carlos Alcaraz since 2019 - and now his prodigy is a Wimbledon champion following an extraordinary defeat of Novak Djokovic yesterday.

But former World No. 1 Juan Carlos Ferrero has an impressive CV in his own right as a tennis player, having won the 2003 French Open, as well as finishing runner-up at the US Open the same year. 

The 43-year-old also helped his home country of Spain win their first Davis Cup title in 2000, and was dubbed 'The Mosquito' thanks to his slight stature and speed as a player, according to the ATP Tour website.

He retired from professional tennis in 2012 before turning his hand to coaching. He started working with Carlos Alcaraz when the Wimbledon champion was just 16 years old and the two have gone from one success to another together. 

Born in 1980, the tennis coach started playing tennis aged seven with his father before leaving home aged 14 to live in his coach's Antonio Martínez's 'humble tennis academy near Valencia', reported The New York Times. 

Carlos Alcaraz celebrates victory with his coach Juan Carlos Ferrero following the Gentlemen's Singles final on day fourteen of the 2023 Wimbledon Championships at the All England Lawn Tennis and Croquet Club

But sadly, when he was just 16, he lost his mother, Rosario, to cancer, the publication revealed.

When she died, his personality was said to have became more withdrawn but his determination to 'try to become the best player in the world' remained.

However, he never appeared to be the most emotional of crowd pleasers during his career partly due to his mother having never liked tennis professionals who were overly excited or energetic. 

But on Sunday night, following Alcaraz's thrilling five-set final against Djokovic, the coach was pictured becoming emotional as he hugged the new Wimbledon champion.

Ferrero married his wife Eva Alonso in summer 2015 in front of 300 guests in a Valencia hotel, according to Lecturas. The couple share three children. 

Following the US Open final in 2022, Ferrero cited his player Carlos Alcaraz and 21-year-old Italian Jannik Sinner as the coming forces in the men's tennis game who will stand above others.

That is based on the two of them having faced off both in New York and at Wimbledon in compelling tussles.

'I said to someone that Sinner and Carlos could dominate the tour for the next 10 years,' reflected Ferrero.

'There are others like Alex Zverev, Dominic Thiem, Casper Ruud, Stefanos Tsitsipas. They're going to have chances to win Slams, for sure. But with respect, that's what I think.'

Worryingly for rivals, Ferrero says Alcaraz is still way short of his potential: 'As I tell him, he's on 60 per cent of his game. He can improve a lot.

'Once you get to No 1, it's not done. Opponents will be very motivated against him. It's like Real Madrid-Barcelona, there's a rivalry that gets you to increase your level.'

Ferrero has spent the last four years dedicating himself to developing Alcaraz, whose rise this season has not come as a shock to him.

'It's a surprise for everybody except me because I trained with him every day. He arrived at the academy when he was 15, he was like spaghetti — very thin.'

Meanwhile, Alcaraz's historic Wimbledon win is worth £2.3million in prize money alone but his defeat of Novak Djokovic will make him 'Gen Z's new hero' and rake in an extra £30million in deals by the end of 2023, experts told MailOnline today.

The 20-year-old's extraordinary performance on the grass in SW19 over the past fortnight has cemented his world number one spot and he is now the natural successor to Djokovic, who he beat in a thrilling five-set final yesterday.

Brand experts say Sunday's win means that he has the 'world at his feet' and the chance to more than quadruple his estimated £7million net worth in the next few months because of his prowess on the court and how humble he is.

Brand and culture expert Nick Ede told MailOnline today that the Spaniard, who still lives with his parents in a £170,000 flat above a kebab shop in southern Spain, will 'easily' make £30million this year as the sponsors roll in. 

PR guru Mark Borkowski says that tennis' global appeal to both men and women means 'we are witnessing the new hero for Gen Z'.
