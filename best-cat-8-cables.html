FAQ

Q: Is an Ethernet cable faster than Wi-Fi?

Yes, Ethernet is faster than Wi-Fi. Ethernet can handle more traffic and is comparable to a multi-lane freeway, whereas Wi-Fi is more like a single-track road.

Q: What are the different Ethernet cable categories?

There are several different Ethernet cable categories. Choosing the right ethernet cable for your needs can be confusing. You can start by forgetting categories one to five as they are old and inefficient.

The ‘e’ in category 5e stands for enhanced. This category is the most commonly used today due to its low cost. However, category 6 supports higher bandwidths and speeds of up to 10Gbps at up to 180 feet. Then there’s Cat 6a. The ‘a’ stands for augmented, and these cables offer better performance than Cat 6 thanks to support for double the bandwidth (500MHz).

Categories 7 and 7a are high-performing but are rare in everyday use thanks to their proprietary connectors and lack of standardization. The 7a cables offer high theoretical speeds but face limitations in real-world scenarios.

Then, of course, Cat 8 cables offer a bandwidth of up to 2GHz and a maximum data rate of up to 40Gbps.

Q: Is a Cat 8 cable better than a Cat 6 cable?

Cat 8 cables have significantly higher speeds and wider bandwidth. They have a top speed of 40Gbps at up to 98 feet (30 meters) as opposed to 10Gbps for Cat 6 (although they perform similarly after that length) and a maximum frequency of 2,000MHz, which is considerably higher than the 250MHz offered by Cat 6. This makes Cat 8 ideal for future high-performance networking. However, for most home network needs today, Cat 6 performs well enough.

Q: Do I need an Ethernet cable to set up a wireless router?

Yes, you’ll need an Ethernet cable with a wireless router. It will connect to the modem so you can bring the internet signal into your home. You’ll need a second Ethernet cable if you want a wired connection to your computer.

Q: Where does an Ethernet cable plug into?

The Ethernet cable plugs into an Ethernet port. This port is a large, squarish socket that you can find on many devices, such as your PC. Your router will have the same port to connect to your modem.
